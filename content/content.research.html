The Computational Molecular Biology group is pursuing 5 primary research topics as well as additional collaborative projects.

Project 1: Evolution of eukaryotic genomes following polyploidization events

Background
Large-scale expansion of genome through polyploidization greatly increases gene repertoire and facilitates emergence of species with novel capabilities, many of which are used in agriculture and biotechnology.

Before reaping the long-term benefits, these polyploids must first cope with the sudden changes in genome.

Our approaches
Many evolutionary mechanisms during the early stages following polyploidization should prioritize genome restabilization.

In collaboration with the University of Tokyo and the Japan Collection of Microorganisms, closely related fungal species that recently underwent genome hybridization were discovered.

Comprehensive analyses of the genome sequences, transcriptome profiles, and chromatin organizations of these species would shed light into the evolutionary processes during the early stages of polyploidization.

We are also interested in the expansion of gene families and signaling pathway components in model plants.

Project 2: Formation and organization of chromosome 3D structured

Background
Genomes are located on chromosomes that fold in 3-dimensional space and form loops, topologically associated domains, and higher-order structures.

These structures can impact gene expression regulation and evolution, for example, by enabling transcription factor sharing acriss genes located in the same topologically associated domain.

Chromosome packing can also limit the accessibility of genes in certain regions from transcription machineries.

Our approaches
Recent models of chromatin folding have been shown to capture many biologically important characteristics of chromosome 3D organization.

We will explore theoretical behaviors of these models through mathematical models and in silico simulations.

Chromosome conformation capture datasets from various organisms will be used to confirm theoretical results and to gain further insight into the folding process of chromatin.

Project 3: Forces governing the distribution and community structure of microorganisms

Background
Microbes possess exceptional dispersability and population sizes that allow them to invade new habitats.

In response to such constant movements and competitions, microbes adopt two general strategies for survival: becoming generalists with larger gene repertoires that let them cope with diverse environments, or becoming specialists with smaller genomes that are optimized for specific environments.

Our approaches
We recently proposed that generalists are responsible for spreading microbial species and genes as they expand across environments.

Further validations of our model, for example, in the context of the spread of antibiotic resistance genes or in the context of disease outbreaks would lead to practical medical applications.

Other broad understandings of microbial community structures and dispersions may be derived from large-sacle publicly available microbiome datasets.

Project 4: Software development and application of machine learning techniques for large-scale biological datasets

Background
Effective data analysis and visualization tools are essential in interpreting large-scale experimental datasets.

Via collaborations with experimental research groups at Chulalongkorn University and other institutes, we have access to a large number of biomedical datasets that need combined expertise in computational and biological sciences to analyze.

Our approaches
We currently supervise undergraduate Computer Engineering students at Chulalongkorn University to analyze large datasets and develop software tools that facilitate such processes.

All techniques, from statistical and mathematical modeling to advanced machine learning, are utilized.

Heavy computations were carried out on our collaborators' high-performance CPU and GPU clusters.

Python, Java, or MATLAB are typically used.

Project 5: Mining of scientific literatures

Background
To conduct effective research, one must rigorously study the literatures and choose the appropriate experimental protocols and data analysis tools.

New research reports and academic software get published at ever-increasing rates that can overwhelm any researcher.

For example, given the sheer number of software tools for processing RNA-seq data, it is time-consuming to evaluate and select the best tools.

Our approaches
Pubmed maintains a database of millions of published biomedical literatures.

Mining of these contents can reveal patterns behind major trends such as research topic popularity or spread of diseases.

We can also automatically and systematically compare software tools that perform similar tasks based on the profiles of their authors, citation trends, and other supporting information.
