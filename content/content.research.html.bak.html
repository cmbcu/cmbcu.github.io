The Computational Molecular Biology group is pursuing the following research topics as well as additional collaborative projects.

Project 1: Evolution of eukaryotic genomes following polyploidization events

Motivation
Large-scale expansion of genome through polyploidization greatly increases gene repertoire and facilitates emergence of species with novel capabilities, many of which are used in agriculture and biotechnology.

Our approaches
We recently discovered a group of closely related yeast hybrids and are now analyzing their genome sequences, transcriptome profiles, and chromatin organizations in order to identify key post-polyploidization evolutionary processes.

We are also interested in how polyploidization direct the expansion of gene families and signaling pathway components in plants.

Project 2: Organization of chromosome 3D structure

Motivation
Genomes are located on chromosomes that fold in 3-dimensional space and form loops, topologically associated domains, and higher-order structures that can impact gene expression regulation and evolution.

Our approaches
Recent models of chromatin folding have been shown to capture many biologically important characteristics of chromosome 3D organization.

We will explore theoretical properties of these models through mathematical models and in silico simulations and validate our findings using publicly available chromatin conformation capture data.

Project 3: The distribution and community structure of microorganisms

Motivation
Microbes possess exceptional dispersability and population sizes that allow them to colonize new habitats. Study of global microbe-environment association pattern should provide insights into the forces and constraints that govern the dynamics of microbial distribution and community structure.

Our approaches
Our recent work suggests that generalist microbes are responsible for spreading microbial species and genes across diverse environments. Further validations of our model, for example, in the context of the spread of antibiotic resistance genes or in the context of disease outbreaks would lead to practical medical applications.

We will mine large-scale microbiome datasets to learn other broad understandings of microbial community structures and dispersions.

Project 4: Software development and application of machine learning for biomedical research

Motivation
Effective data analysis and visualization tools are essential for interpreting any large-scale experimental dataset.

Many research groups at Chulalongkorn University as well as our collaborators generate large amount of data that need combined expertise in computational and biological sciences to analyze.

Our approaches
We utilize statistical and machine learning techniques, with access to high performance CPU and GPU clusters.

Python and MATLAB are primarily used, followed by Java and C++ knowledge

Project 5: Mining of scientific literatures

Motivation
To conduct effective research, one must rigorously study the literatures and choose the appropriate experimental protocols and data analysis tools. However, new research reports and academic software get published at ever-increasing rates.

For example, given the sheer number of software tools for processing RNA-seq data, it is time-consuming to evaluate and select the best tools.

Our approaches
Pubmed maintains a database of millions of published biomedical literatures.

We are interested in developing an automatic curation system that can capture and track current global research interests as well as identify promising bioinformatics tools.
